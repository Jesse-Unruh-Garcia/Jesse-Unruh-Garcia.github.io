Mountain Goat (Oreamnos americanus)
January 26th, 2006

Not big fans of the goats.
One of the goats kicked me in the freaking shin this morning. Caught me by surprise. Chased it down for about ten minutes before I gave up. They’re cute and all but I can’t wait till they get them all out of here. They’re invasive, not supposed to be in the Olympic Peninsula at all. Almost makes you feel bad, since they don’t even know that they’re breaking the place down. Little guys just kick around because it’s all they know.

Last summer one of the goats rammed their head against Henry’s shin. Broke it hard. I carried Henry for three hours to a rest site and they helicoptered him out. Classic tough guy. Gave me a thumbs up and blew me a kiss as he sailed through the sky. Tom-Cruise-style sexy guy.

Transportation.

Eighty years ago, farmers brought their goats over to Washington State and let them wander about. These goats thrived in the steep hills and nutritious grasses of the Olympic Peninsula, and so a small number of them bred and created communities here. Two became four became eight became sixteen, and so on. But with it, the goats learned that by kicking and headbutting, they could steal the resources of their surroundings. Marmots left the goats alone, because they were too big. Bears left the goats alone, because the goats could run faster than them. Deer left the goats alone, because they appeared as no threat. And so, the goats kicked and headbutted all on their own. They occupied a space that wasn’t theirs to know, and never knowing about their real home.

They cycled around, and they kicked themselves, and they kicked me.

One time I watched the goats so closely I lost track of time. I stayed by their sides, slowly creeping closer and closer. Ever so often, one would come to kick me. But their kicks grew less frequent, and they grew more comfortable. And then I could get so close I could touch, and make small observations of the group of goats. I wrote their actions and their mannerisms and the way they interacted with the world and each other. I became their actions and their mannerisms. I slept by their sides, and forgot about the tower. They thought I was a goat, and for a small while, I thought I was too.

Common Name: Mountain Goat

Scientific Name: Oreamnos americanus

Translation: American Rock Lamb (So close!)
