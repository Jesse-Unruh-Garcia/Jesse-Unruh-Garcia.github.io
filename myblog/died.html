Funeral Contest

Henry’s funeral was so large. I didn’t know so many people could love one person. It made me feel bad. Thinking - no - knowing that I loved him more than all of them. And I don’t say that to be rude or self-important. It was a competition. And I won it. I demolished it. And the fuckers like his mom or dad or sister who think they beat me are wrong. I was the Michael Jordan of loving Henry and they could all eat it. It’s not my fault the tournament was cut short.

They asked me if I wanted to do the eulogy. They were kind and comforting and put their hands on my shoulders and rubbed my back and bought me dinners. And I said yes. And I went up to the podium. And there were a million different eyes that all loved him and loved me through his extension. And at first it felt suffocating, and their guises were pulling the plug. And eventually it felt like a weighted blanket, and I could get the few words I had left. And I spoke for twenty seconds, and the speech was bare.


But they all loved me through his extension.


Months after the incident in the park, I brought Henry’s ashes to the moss. I scattered them, and when I turned I saw the hares and the goats and the moss. The daisies and the watchful eyes and the impressions of man. And I knew then I could make my wish. And I didn’t fear that it would corrupt me.
On the precipice.
