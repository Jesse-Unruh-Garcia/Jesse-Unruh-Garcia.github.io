Clubmoss, Dicranum, Sphagnum, Liverwort, Hylocomniums, Rhizominiums, Clamaciums, Plagiothecium, Fontinalises, and likely a dozen other mosses cling themselves to the pine trees in the park. Sometimes they overflow so heavily that they feel like they are the trees themselves. In many ways they are, as many of the mosses have a symbiotic relationship with the trees, providing moisture and nitrogen in exchange for a place to call home.

I walk through the halls during my free time, some of the biggest patches are a bit of a hike from the tower, so I have to make evenings of it. They cling to my hiking boots and to my hands and to my overalls as I walk around with my pen and paper. Like the moss wants to hang on to me too, make me part of the hall and part of the trees and part of the peninsula. It feels cold and wet in the winters, but hydrating. It feels soft and natural and I press my hands against the walls and watch it make an imprint. My imprint. Like giving a million tiny hugs to a million tiny grasses.
